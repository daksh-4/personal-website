Proust and Neuroscience

April 2026
Home Essays About

I was attending lectures and Prof introduced associative memory with an excerpt from Proust's "In Search of Lost Time".

The narrator is actually at home, in Book 1 (Swann’s Way). He takes a bite of a petite madeleine dipped in warm lime-blossom tea. What follows isn't an instant flashback, but a sudden, overwhelming sense of joy. The specific memory takes considerable, conscious effort to excavate (and is only described to the reader several pages later), but the emotion arrives instantly. Eventually, he realises the taste reminds him of Sunday mornings in the fictional town of Combray, when his aunt Léonie would give him a piece of her madeleine dipped in tea.

"And suddenly the memory returns. The taste was that of the little crumb of madeleine which on Sunday mornings at Combray (because on those mornings I did not go out before church-time), when I went to say good day to her in her bedroom, my aunt Léonie used to give me, dipping it first in her own cup of real or of lime-flower tea."

This was very interesting and led me down a rabbit hole of the unique connections between the field of Neuroscience and Marcel Proust.

From the age of nine, Proust suffered from severe asthma. Today, we know asthma is a respiratory condition, but in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, it was classified as a "nervous disease." It fell under the umbrella of neurasthenia: a fashionable, catch-all medical diagnosis for a wide array of unexplained physical and mental symptoms.

Alongside his asthma, he suffered from insomnia, digestive ailments, headaches, dizziness, slurred speech and back pain. Because his father, Dr. Adrien Proust, was a prominent professor of medicine who had actually written on neurasthenia, Marcel wasn't dismissed as insane. Instead, he was thoroughly medicalised, placed directly into the hands of the Parisian medical elite.

Proust consulted the most famous neurologists of the Belle Époque such as Édouard Brissaud, Jules Dejerine and Joseph Babinski. Because asthma was considered a neurological disorder, these were the appropriate specialists for his care.

But the most fascinating connection between Proust and science is found in Dr. Paul Sollier. A follower of the famed neurologist Jean-Martin Charcot, Sollier specialised in memory. He treated Proust using an "isolation cure" that relied heavily on triggering reviviscences (emotional revivals). Sollier’s therapy involved trying to induce patients to reproduce entire emotional states from their past.

When In Search of Lost Time was published, modern neuroscience didn't exist. Yet, Proust mapped the architecture of human memory in a way that was unique to the time and part of the reason why his books have such acclaim.

For a long time, scientists thought memories were hard coded and didn't change over time. Modern neuroscience discovered memory reconsolidation, which shows that every time we recall a memory, it becomes unstable and is often altered by our current emotional state. He often wrote about how our present feelings colour our past memories. In the novel, the narrator's memories of people (like Swann or Albertine) constantly shift based on new information or his current mood.

Neuroscientists also study a phenomenon called "neural adaptation" or habituation, where neurons stop firing in response to a constant stimulus. Proust wrote extensively about the "anaesthetic effect of habit." He believed that our daily routines dull our senses to the beauty of the world. For Proust, involuntary memory (like the madeleine) and art are the only things that pierce through this habituation, forcing our brains to "wake up" and perceive reality fresh again.

He also understood that retrieval cues (like a taste or smell) can induce recall and that these involuntary memories carry a vividness that voluntary memory completely lacks. It took cognitive psychologists nearly 80 years to systematically investigate and validate these ideas. Proust’s focus on the senses of taste and smell was particularly prescient. We now know that olfaction (smell) is wired differently than our other senses. While sight and sound are routed through the thalamus before reaching the cortex, smell bypasses the thalamus entirely. It has a direct pathway to the hippocampus and the amygdala.

Modern neuroscientists have developed experimental paradigms literally called "The Proust Machine" to study how different sensory modalities trigger autobiographical memories. Cognitive psychologists even debate whether Proust deserves credit for predating the modern concept of "episodic memory."

Neurology gave Proust the framework to explore the depths of human memory, and a century later, Proust’s literature continues to inspire the questions neuroscientists ask about the human brain. Science may have finally caught up with Marcel Proust, but we still need his literature to explain how it feels.
